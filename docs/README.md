# 🎮 Control Surface & Fuselage Actuation Roadmap

## Overview

OpenFuselage-UAV is developed using an incremental engineering approach.

Instead of designing a complex mechanically rotating fuselage from the beginning, the project first validates the flight concept using only conventional aerodynamic control surfaces (elevons).

This allows the flight controller, control algorithms, and airframe dynamics to be tested independently before introducing additional mechanical complexity.

The project is therefore divided into two development phases.

---

# Phase 1 – Aerodynamic Fuselage Rotation (Proof of Concept)

The first prototype relies entirely on aerodynamic forces generated by the rear elevons.

No motors or rotating mechanisms are used during this stage.

The primary objective is to demonstrate that the aircraft can rotate its fuselage and maintain stable flight using only aerodynamic control.

## Step 1 – Rotate the Fuselage

To rotate the fuselage around its longitudinal axis (Roll), the elevons move in **opposite directions** (differential deflection).

```
Left Elevon     ↑
Right Elevon    ↓
```

The resulting aerodynamic moment slowly rolls the entire fuselage.

An onboard **IMU (Gyroscope + Accelerometer)** continuously measures:

- Current roll angle
- Roll rate
- Angular acceleration

The flight controller uses this information to determine when the desired roll angle has been reached.

---

## Step 2 – Change Flight Direction

Once the fuselage reaches the desired orientation, the elevons switch to **collective deflection**.

Both elevons move in the same direction.

```
Left Elevon     ↑
Right Elevon    ↑
```

or

```
Left Elevon     ↓
Right Elevon    ↓
```

This produces the aerodynamic force required to change the aircraft's flight path.

The elevons are therefore used for two different purposes:

1. Rotate the fuselage.
2. Control the aircraft's direction after the rotation is complete.

Although this method is less efficient than a dedicated mechanical system, it is simple, lightweight, and ideal for validating the flight concept.

---

# Phase 2 – Motorized Fuselage Rotation (Future Development)

Once the aerodynamic concept has been successfully demonstrated, the project will transition to an actively driven fuselage.

A high-torque electric motor combined with a compact reduction gearbox will rotate the fuselage directly.

The IMU will continue to provide real-time orientation feedback, while the flight controller commands the motor until the requested roll angle is reached.

In this configuration:

- The motor performs all fuselage rotation.
- The IMU measures the actual orientation.
- The flight controller closes the control loop.
- The elevons are no longer responsible for rolling the aircraft.

Instead, the elevons become dedicated aerodynamic control surfaces used only for:

- Pitch control
- Directional corrections
- Flight stabilization

Separating fuselage rotation from aerodynamic steering is expected to improve efficiency, increase control authority, and simplify flight control.

---

# Engineering Challenge

The motorized solution introduces significant mechanical challenges.

Adding a motor, gearbox, bearings, and structural reinforcement to the rotating fuselage section increases the mass at the rear of the aircraft.

This directly affects:

- Center of Gravity (CG)
- Moment of Inertia (MoI)
- Structural loads
- Flight stability
- Power consumption

The entire airframe must therefore be redesigned to maintain a proper mass distribution.

Batteries, avionics, and other heavy components may need to be relocated toward the nose to compensate for the additional rear mass.

For this reason, the motorized system is intentionally postponed until the aerodynamic concept has been fully validated.

---

# Development Strategy

The project follows a clear engineering roadmap.

**Phase 1**

- Validate the aerodynamic concept.
- Develop the flight controller.
- Tune the control algorithms.
- Verify stable fuselage rotation using only elevons.

**Phase 2**

- Introduce a motorized rotating fuselage.
- Separate fuselage rotation from flight control.
- Optimize the mechanical design.
- Rebalance the aircraft's mass distribution.
- Improve efficiency and flight performance.

---

# Why This Approach?

Developing a completely new flight control concept and a mechanical fuselage rotation system at the same time would make debugging extremely difficult.

If the aircraft became unstable, it would be impossible to determine whether the problem originated from:

- the control software,
- the aerodynamic design,
- the mechanical rotation system,
- or the changing mass distribution.

By validating the aerodynamic concept first, each subsystem can be tested independently.

Once stable flight has been achieved, the mechanical rotation system can be introduced with much lower technical risk.

This staged development process reduces complexity, simplifies testing, and makes future improvements significantly easier.

---

## Long-Term Goal

The long-term objective of OpenFuselage-UAV is to create a tailless aircraft with an actively rotating fuselage that combines:

- Simple mechanical design
- Efficient aerodynamic control
- Stable autonomous flight
- Modular hardware architecture
- Open-source development

Each development phase builds upon the previous one, allowing the project to evolve from a simple proof of concept into a fully motorized rotating-fuselage UAV.
